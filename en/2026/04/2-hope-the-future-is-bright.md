Hope: The Future Is Bright
By Brivael Le Pogam, Posted on April 3, 2026


The kid in front of the screen
I was 15 years old, with no money, no network, no prestigious school in sight. Just a computer and an obsession: building things. I taught myself to code alone, at night, reading English documentation I only half understood. My first real project was an online multiplayer game. 50,000 players ended up playing it. Nobody had given me permission. Nobody had validated me. I just did it.
That is the first lesson life taught me, and probably the most important one: the world belongs to those who build without asking for permission.
Today I am co-founder and CTO of Argil, a Y Combinator-backed startup building the infrastructure for AI video generation. We serve tens of thousands of users in more than 40 countries. We have raised funds, we hire, we sign paychecks. And every morning, I feel exactly the same sensation as that 15-year-old kid in front of his screen: the thrill of making something that did not exist the day before.
If I tell you this, it is not out of narcissism. It is because my journey is living proof of the thesis I will defend in this article: the future is bright, as long as we let people build.
The France that punishes those who act
Before being optimistic, you have to be lucid.
France has a structural problem. It is not a talent problem; we have plenty. It is not a capital problem; it exists. It is a philosophy problem. The French system rests on an implicit belief, deeply rooted in its institutions: wealth is suspect, business is guilty until proven innocent, and the state knows better than you what is good for you.
When you start a company in France, you are not only fighting the market. You are fighting an administrative apparatus that considers you first as a taxpayer, then as an employer to be monitored, and only as a last resort, perhaps, as a creator of value. Taxes, regulations, forms, inspections: everything is designed as if entrepreneurship were a dangerous activity that must be framed, regulated, and contained.
We know the result. The best leave. Capital flees. And those who stay must deploy colossal energy just to exist, energy that should go into innovation, product, and growth.
I am not saying this from a Parisian café while sipping an espresso. I live it. Every month. Every paycheck. Every interaction with the administration.
Hayek and the lesson of humility
Reading Friedrich Hayek (The Road to Serfdom) gave me the words to describe what I felt intuitively.
Hayek formulated an idea of formidable power: knowledge is dispersed. No individual, no committee, no ministry can concentrate in one place all the information needed to make the right economic and social decisions. Relevant knowledge is scattered across millions of brains, local contexts, and particular situations. The free market is not a dogma; it is a mechanism for processing information. The price of a good is a signal. Supply and demand are a language. And every central intervention that distorts these signals produces effects that nobody wanted and nobody controls.
When I look at French economic policy through this lens, everything becomes clear. The wealth tax does not punish "the rich"; it destroys a signal. The minimum wage does not protect "workers"; it prevents some of them from accessing the market. The social security system for the self-employed does not secure "independents"; it drowns them in complexity that only the largest can absorb.
This is not cruelty. It is lucidity. And lucidity is the first duty of anyone who claims to want to improve the world.
Girard, or why everyone thinks the same thing
There is a reason these ideas are a minority in France. And it is not because they are false.
René Girard described a fundamental mechanism of human psychology: mimetic desire. We do not desire things for what they are. We desire them because others desire them. And what applies to objects applies to opinions.
In France, the intellectual consensus is anti-liberal. Not because the arguments were weighed and liberalism lost. But because the media, academic, and political world functions like an echo chamber where it is socially costly to think differently. Saying "I am a liberal" at a Parisian dinner is to exclude yourself. So people stay silent. And the silence of some reinforces the conviction of others.
Girard teaches us that most convictions are not convictions. They are disguised conformism. Intellectual courage begins when you accept having an opinion that pleases no one in the room.
Taleb and the art of not dying
Nassim Nicholas Taleb added a decisive piece to my framework: antifragility.
Fragile systems are those that try to eliminate volatility. Robust systems tolerate it. Antifragile systems benefit from it. The entrepreneur is antifragile by nature. Every failure is information. Every crisis is a filter that eliminates the weak and strengthens the adaptable. The free market, with its apparent brutality, is a machine for producing collective antifragility.
The French welfare state does the opposite. It absorbs short-term shocks by piling up long-term fragility. Public debt is volatility swept under the rug. Subsidies to dying sectors are therapeutic obstinacy on fragile structures. Job protection, when excessive, is a prohibition on the system reconfiguring itself.
Taleb taught me that wanting to control everything is the purest form of fragility. And that those who build the future are those who accept chaos, not those who spend their lives trying to eliminate it.
Nietzsche, or the duty to create
One last brick remains. The most personal.
Nietzsche wrote that the superior man is the one who creates his own values. Not the one who inherits a moral system, not the one who follows the herd, but the one who looks at the world as it is and decides, in full consciousness, what he wants to make of it.
When I left a comfortable position to start Argil, I did not make a "career choice." I made an existential choice. I decided that I preferred risk and creation to security and repetition. Not because it is smarter. Because it is the only way to live that seems worthy to me.
And that is the real techno-optimist manifesto. It is not a prediction. It is a choice. The choice to believe that human beings, when left free, when not crushed under the weight of norms and mediocrity, are capable of extraordinary things.
The future is bright
Now let us talk about what is coming.
Artificial intelligence is the deepest rupture since the printing press. And I am not saying that as a startup pitch. I say it as someone who builds these systems every day, line of code after line of code.
What AI will do is not "replace jobs." It is to multiply the capacity of every individual. A single developer today can build what required a team of twenty people five years ago. A content creator can produce professional-quality videos without a studio, without a team, without a budget. An entrepreneur in a village in Senegal has access to the same tools as an engineer in Silicon Valley.
That is real democratization. Not the forced redistribution of finite wealth. The infinite multiplication of the capacity to create value.
And AI is only the beginning. Longevity is progressing. Gene therapies are arriving. Clean energy is becoming competitive. Access to knowledge is nearly universal. Every fundamental indicator of the human condition, infant mortality, extreme poverty, access to education, life expectancy, has been pointing in the right direction for decades.
But nobody tells you that. Because optimism does not get clicks. Because fear is a business model. Because merchants of decline need you to be terrified to justify their existence.
Degrowth is collective suicide
It must be said clearly: degrowth is an ideology of death.
Not immediate physical death. Slow death. Resignation. Renunciation. Telling people they must "produce less, consume less, innovate less" is telling them that the best is behind us. That humanity is a problem whose solution is to fade away.
That is the exact opposite of everything history teaches us. Every great period of human progress has been driven by expansion, innovation, and risk-taking. The Renaissance, the Industrial Revolution, the digital age: each time, the prophets of the end of the world were wrong. And each time, humanity found solutions that nobody had imagined.
The climate emergency is real. But the answer cannot be rationing. The answer is liberated human ingenuity. Nuclear power. Fusion. Carbon capture. Next-generation materials. The solutions exist or are emerging. They must be funded, developed, and deployed. Not a return to candlelight while congratulating ourselves on our virtue.
What I am building, and why
Argil is not just a startup. It is an embodiment of everything I have just written.
We take a breakthrough technology, AI video generation. We make it accessible to people who could not afford to produce professional video content. We give them tools that multiply their creativity without demanding technical skills they do not have. We build this from Paris, with a team of twelve people, for the whole world.
That is optimism in action. Not speeches. Lines of code. Users. Lives changed.
Every day, I see creators in Africa, Asia, and Latin America produce content they could never have imagined two years ago. Every day, the asymmetry between those who have means and those who have talent shrinks. And every day, I tell myself that this movement is irreversible.
A call
I am not writing this article to convince the convinced.
I am writing it for those who doubt. For those who sense that something is wrong in the ambient discourse but dare not say it. For the silent builders who build in their corner while commentators profit from their work to finance their utopias.
The future is bright. Not because everything is fine. Because the tools to solve problems have never been so powerful, and free individuals have never had so many means to act.
We just have to stop preventing them.
Hope is not naivety. It is the most radical form of courage. It is looking at the world as it is, with all its brutality and all its beauty, and deciding that we will make it better. Not by controlling it. By liberating it.
The future will be incredibly beautiful and positive. And nobody will stop us from building it.
