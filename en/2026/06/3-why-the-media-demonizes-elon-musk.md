Why the Media Demonizes Elon Musk
by Brivael Le Pogam, posted on June 22nd, 2026

There is a riddle at the center of modern public life, and almost no one says it out loud.
Here is a man who put the first privately built rocket into orbit, then landed it standing upright on a drone ship in the middle of the ocean. A man who forced the entire global auto industry ΓÇö every legacy giant that swore it couldn't be done ΓÇö to go electric. A man who connects the disconnected corners of the planet from low orbit, who is building the machines that may take our species off this single fragile rock.
And the same press that should be writing him into the lineage of Edison and the Wright brothers spends its days painting him as a villain.
Why?
The honest answer has nothing to do with a tweet. It has to do with what he is ΓÇö and what he threatens.
1. The Builder
Strip away the memes and the noise, and Musk's story is the oldest and best one we have: a builder who arrives with almost nothing and refuses to wait for permission.
In the mid-90s he and his brother founded Zip2, a company that helped newspapers go online. There was no fund, no comfort, no safety net. He slept in the office and showered at the YMCA. When Compaq bought Zip2, his cut was around twenty-two million dollars.
A normal person retires on a beach. Musk took nearly all of it and poured it into X.com ΓÇö an online bank, an absurd idea at the time ΓÇö which merged with a competitor to become PayPal. When PayPal sold to eBay, his share was something like 180 million dollars.
That is the moment most people would call "winning." For Musk it was simply ammunition. He turned right around and bet it on the two least fundable industries on Earth.
2. All In
In 2002 he founded SpaceX ΓÇö to compete with governments in rocketry. Shortly after came Tesla, to compete with the most capital-intensive incumbents in the world. Two ventures, each of which any sane investor would have called suicidal.
By 2008 the bill came due. SpaceX had failed three launches in a row; one more failure meant the company died. Tesla was burning cash with no production at scale and a financial system in free fall around it. Musk was, by his own account, splitting his last reserves between the two companies and choosing ΓÇö week to week ΓÇö which one got to keep breathing.
He has described lying awake wondering whether he would lose both at once. This was not the cushioned risk of a man playing with house money. This was a man who had put his entire fortune back on the table and was about to lose it all.
Then, in the final weeks of 2008, SpaceX's fourth launch reached orbit. NASA awarded the contract that kept the company alive. Tesla closed its financing in the last hours of the year. He had walked to the very edge of total ruin ΓÇö and stepped back.
Remember that the next time someone tells you he was simply born lucky. Luck does not stand at a 2008 ledger and choose which of its children gets to live.
3. The Ascent
What followed is the part everyone knows because it was impossible to hide.
Tesla, the company Detroit laughed at, eventually became worth more than the legacy automakers combined. SpaceX became the dominant launch provider on the planet, hauling more mass to orbit than entire nation-states. Starlink lit up the sky and the battlefield. And the kid who showered at the YMCA became, by most measures, the richest man in the world.
Here is the first clue to the hatred: he did it without the establishment's blessing. He did not rise through the cozy ladder of credentialed gatekeepers. He bulldozed past them. And institutions never forgive the man who proves they were unnecessary.
4. The Democrat Who Took the Consensus Seriously
Now the part that gets erased.
For most of his public life, Musk was a man of the center-left. He voted Democrat. He supported Obama. He was the climate-conscious, electric-car, save-the-planet billionaire ΓÇö the good kind, in the eyes of the press that now despises him.
He believed the consensus because he is, fundamentally, an engineer: he assumed the people running the institutions were operating in good faith and optimizing for the truth, the way a good system should. He hadn't yet examined the machinery underneath the slogans. He took the moral language at face value, because at the time he had no reason not to.
As he later put it: he didn't leave the left ΓÇö the left left him. The man didn't move. The ground did.
5. The Rupture
The turn was not abstract. It became personal.
Musk has spoken publicly, and bitterly, about becoming estranged from one of his children, who is transgender. In his own telling, he feels he was not fully informed about decisions made while that child was a minor, and he blames what he calls the "woke mind virus" for the loss of the relationship. He has described it as one of the most painful things in his life.
You don't have to share his framing to understand its effect on him. Whatever the truth of that family's private pain, he experienced it as ideology reaching into his own home and taking something from him. For a man who solves problems by tracing them to their root cause, that was the moment the abstract became concrete. He stopped accepting the moral language at face value and started asking where it actually came from, and who it actually served.
6. He Did the Homework
This is the part his enemies most want to caricature, so be precise about it.
Musk did what engineers do: he went looking for the mechanism. He concluded that a great deal of what presents itself as spontaneous moral progress is in fact organized ΓÇö that there is an ecosystem of activist NGOs, sympathetic media, and ideologically captured institutions that move in coordination, fund the same causes, and police the same boundaries. He has named names, including pointed and controversial claims about George Soros and the network of foundations he funds, and has argued that a self-described compassion is often a lever for power.
Whether you accept every link in that chain is beside the point. The decisive shift is that he stopped treating the dominant ideology as the weather ΓÇö a neutral fact of the climate ΓÇö and started treating it as a thing built by people for reasons. Once you see a structure as engineered, you stop being awed by it. You start asking who designed it, what it optimizes for, and how to route around it.
That single shift in posture is what makes him dangerous to the people who benefit from never being examined.
7. The Man With the Means to Clean House
And here is why the danger is not theoretical.
Most people who reach this conclusion are powerless to act on it. They can mutter at the television. Musk arrived at it while being the richest and arguably most influential individual alive ΓÇö and while owning the public square itself. He bought the platform, tore out the moderation regime he believed was rigging the discourse, and turned it into something the legacy gatekeepers no longer control.
He cannot be silenced by an advertiser boycott, because he doesn't need the advertisers. He cannot be brought to heel by an unfavorable news cycle, because he speaks directly to hundreds of millions. He cannot be quietly de-platformed, because he owns the platform. For the first time in a long time, someone with the resources, the reach, and the appetite for risk is in a position to actually pry open the machinery and let people look inside.
That is a nightmare for everyone whose authority depended on no one ever looking.
8. The Smear With a Mechanism
So they reached for the most powerful weapon in the entire arsenal.
Not "we disagree with him." Not "his policies are wrong." The word they reach for is fascist. Nazi. They seize on an awkward gesture, a clumsy phrasing, an out-of-context clip, and they run it on a loop until the association is welded into the public mind.
His defenders argue ΓÇö and it is hard to dismiss them ΓÇö that this is not sloppiness but strategy. Because the "Nazi" smear is the one accusation that switches off thought entirely. Everyone wants to believe they would have been the brave soul who stopped Hitler. Everyone wants to be the hero of that story. So if you can convince enough people that this man is the next great evil, you don't just discredit him ΓÇö you transform attacking him into a moral duty. You turn ordinary, decent people into a mob and let them feel righteous about it.
That is the part worth sitting with. The people deploying that word are not stupid. They are professionals. They know exactly what the accusation does and exactly why it works. To level the gravest charge in human memory at a man you know to be something else is not an error of judgment ΓÇö it is a choice. And choices, eventually, get remembered. The ledger doesn't close just because the news cycle does.
The Real Reason
Pull all of it together and the riddle dissolves.
They do not hate Elon Musk despite his achievements. They hate him because of what those achievements expose.
He is the living refutation of crony capitalism ΓÇö the cozy, managed system where success flows not from building better things but from regulatory moats, lobbying access, subsidy capture, and the right friendships. That system wears the costume of "capitalism" while functioning as its opposite: a protection racket for incumbents, held together by red tape and quiet corruption, where the press, the regulators, and the entrenched giants all scratch one another's backs.
Musk is the other thing. The real thing. Competitive, risk-soaked, creative-destruction capitalism ΓÇö the kind that drags the future forward whether the incumbents consent or not. He landed rockets the defense primes said were impossible. He built cars the regulators' favorite automakers swore couldn't sell. He routed around the gatekeepers at every step and won anyway.
A man like that cannot be bought with ad revenue, cannot be tamed by regulation, cannot be flattered into compliance by access. He is outside every lever they have ever used to keep powerful people in line.
That is the whole story. He is hated not because he is the villain they describe, but because he is the one variable their entire system was not built to control. And on some level, they know it.
The demonization isn't a verdict on Elon Musk.
It's a confession about them.
