Soros Did Not Prolong the Open Society
by Brivael Le Pogam, posted on June 23rd, 2026

The other day, I presented to you the French architect of globalism: Attali.

Today, the same software — but on a global scale.

George Soros.

And Soros is an even purer case than Attali. Because Attali never claimed to be the heir of the man who had refuted him in advance.

Soros did. In black and white. That's the whole affair.

Let's go over his life quickly, because it matters.

Budapest, 1930. A Jewish kid who survives the Nazi occupation with false papers. He emigrates to London. He enters the London School of Economics. And there, he has a teacher: Karl Popper.

Then finance. The Quantum Fund. And 1992: he short-sells the British pound, breaks the Bank of England, and pockets about a billion dollars in a single day. "The man who broke the Bank of England."

With this fortune, he builds the most powerful philanthropic machine on the planet: the Open Society Foundations.

Stop on the name. Open Society.

He took it directly from Popper. From his book: The Open Society and Its Enemies. The most powerful text ever written against the planners.

Soros kept the cover. And he threw away the content.

Because what does Popper really say?

That no one holds the final truth. So we reform through small touches, step by step, keeping everything correctable. That institutions must remain open to criticism. And above all — his deepest target — that historicism must be killed: this belief that History has a meaning, a direction, which would authorize sacrificing real people of today for an abstract better future.

Popper had seen where that leads. He named it the enemy.

And what did the student of the master's lesson do? Exactly the opposite.

1992 already tells you. Soros becomes a legend by proving that a single actor, with enough leverage and conviction, can bend an entire system to his reading. In the markets, that's the game. The problem begins when one applies this psychology to societies.

Billions poured to reshape, from the top, institutions, NGOs, programs, media, in dozens of countries. This is not the modest and appropriate reform by the people that Popper spoke of. This is the grand design.

And when he writes about immigration, in 2015-2016, he writes it himself, publicly: his plan for the European migration crisis. The EU must welcome a fixed large number each year, finance it through common debt, etc.

Put the substance aside for a second. Look at the form.

It is the exact gesture of Attali, on a world scale: decide, from above, for peoples who have never been consulted, what is good for them. The planner does not convince. He designs.

And in the face of failure? Always the same conclusion. Never "I was wrong". Always "the plan was not applied strongly enough". More governance. More institutions above the peoples.

To be fair, as with Attali. Soros is not a movie villain. He is a survivor, a brilliant man, sincerely convinced of making the world better. It is precisely this that makes him the perfect textbook case. Our enemies are not bad people — they are bad ideas. And this one is the purest, and the most dangerous of all: that the world is piloted from above, and that your freedom is a variable to adjust.

So let's say it.

Soros did not prolong the open society. He became, almost literally, the figure against which Popper's book was written. He kept the word "open" and built the closure: a design without borders, decided without you, impermeable to your objection, certain of its direction.

Popper offered a method to correct our errors without bloodshed.

Soros offers a sense of History to obey.

The first leaves you free. The second finds you useful.

You know which side I build on.

To work.
