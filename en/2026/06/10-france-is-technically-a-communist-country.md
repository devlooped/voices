France Is Technically a Communist Country
by Brivael Le Pogam, posted on June 24th, 2026

France is technically a communist country. And I am going to prove it to you.

In 1989, the Berlin Wall fell and we popped the champagne. We declared victory, explained to the children that communism was over, that the market had won, that freedom had won, that History itself had ended right there. Except it hadn't. 1989 wasn't the death of communism, it was the fall of a symbol. The tanks left, the red flags were put away, the portraits of Lenin ended up in museums — but the idea never died. It simply understood that it could no longer return with tanks. So it came back differently: softer, more polite, more smiling. A soft neo-communism.

Communism version 1.0 was brutal: the gulag, the political police, shortages, famine, walls to prevent people from fleeing. Version 2.0 has no gulag — it has something better. It obtained something the USSR never managed to wrest from its people: your consent. You think you are living in a free country, in a market economy, in a capitalist democracy. And yet, when you look coldly at the numbers, the picture that emerges is quite different.

Piece 1: The State weighs heavier than in the USSR

In 2025, France's public spending represents 57.2 percent of GDP according to INSEE. Read that carefully: not 30 percent, not 40 percent, but 57.2 percent. More than half of all the wealth produced by the country passes through the State. It is the second highest ratio in the entire eurozone, just behind Finland, while the OECD average hovers around 43.6 percent and the United States are far behind. In its final years, the USSR was around 50 percent public spending as a share of GDP. In other words, France in 2025 does better than the Soviet Union at capturing wealth through the state. A country that thinks of itself as capitalist captures, proportionally, more wealth than the empire that made collectivism its state religion.

Let's be honest about the limits of the comparison. The USSR was a command economy where the State owned most of the means of production, so the ratio does not measure exactly the same thing. But when the State captures 57 percent of GDP, levies 43.6 percent in mandatory contributions, carries a debt of 115.6 percent of GDP, and runs a 5.1 percent deficit every year, you are no longer in a market economy. You are in an administered economy that does not dare say its name.

Piece 2: No entrepreneur makes a real exit here

The real test of a capitalist ecosystem comes down to one question: do people build fortunes by creating value here? In France the honest answer is no. We have unicorns on paper, about thirty of them, but look at what happens to them. Dataiku moved its headquarters to the United States. Algolia went to San Francisco. Aircall and Talend left too. Pasqal, the quantum crown jewel born in Palaiseau, chose Nasdaq for its stock market listing. Today there are about 46 French unicorn founders settled in the United States, versus only 18 in France. We train the champions here, we grow them here, and at the moment of exit they fly away.

Why? Corporate tax at 25 percent when Ireland practices 12.5 percent and the United States 21 percent. Capital gains taxation around 30 percent before even talking about the rest. And above all, a cultural logic where success becomes suspect. A capitalist country rewards value creation. A communist country punishes it. Guess which one we live in.

Piece 3: Even scale-up founders live modestly

This is the point no one dares to say out loud. Go talk to French founders who built companies of 50, 100, 200 people, who created hundreds of jobs, who took crazy risks. People who in San Francisco or Austin would already be financially free. Look at their real standard of living: maybe a nice apartment, a decent car, normal vacations, but not the patrimonial sovereignty that their work should have produced. Between social charges, corporate tax, income tax, flat tax, wealth tax, and social contributions, the system is calibrated for one thing only: that no one really takes off.

And that is exactly the communist project. Not misery for all, but leveling for all. They leave you comfortable. They never leave you sovereign.

Piece 4: We finance public services that no longer work

Here is the paradox that should wake us all up. We have the highest level of public spending in the West, and our public services are collapsing. Hospitals are cracking, emergency rooms are closing, caregivers are fleeing. Justice is clogged and its delays are unworthy. Schools are plummeting in international rankings despite a colossal budget. Insecurity is taking root in areas where, twenty years ago, it was unthinkable. We pay the price of a premium Nordic system and we receive the service of a bankrupt system.

Where does the money go? Into the machine. Into the bureaucracy, into the layers, into the agencies, into the committees, into the standards, into all those people whose job is to control those who produce. This is the exact signature of communism: maximum spending, minimum results, and an administration that grows on the ruins of what it was supposed to serve. The USSR also had magnificent five-year plans on paper and empty shelves in reality.

Piece 5: The European Union ticks all the boxes

Above all this stands a higher level: a supranational entity with a non-elected central executive body, the Commission, that produces directives, standards, harmonization, and planning disguised as strategy. It decides for 450 million people what they have the right to grow, to build, to store, to say, to finance. GDPR, the AI Act, the Green Deal, thousands of pages of compliance. A twelve-person startup must appoint a DPO, fill out compliance tables, justify its carbon footprint and verify its ethical alignment, while across the way people are building SpaceX, Nvidia, OpenAI or Anduril.

A system that centrally plans, massively redistributes, regulates everything, distrusts the sovereign individual, and whose leaders are not directly chosen by the peoples bears a name, and it is not capitalism.

The truth is we have been scammed

They sold us a free country, a market economy, a startup nation. And they delivered to us, in silence, without revolution, without coup d'etat, without a single tank in the street, a system that captures 57 percent of GDP, that weighs heavier than the USSR, that drives away its best builders, that impoverishes those who create, that finances services that no longer work, and that places above it a supranational bureaucracy that no one really elected.

Communism was not defeated in 1989: it mutated. It took off the uniform and put on a suit. It replaced the gulag with regulation, scarcity with leveling, censorship with moderation, the political commissar with the ethics committee. And the worst part is that it almost contaminated the United States too: the campuses, the media, tech, corporate culture. Over there the awakening has begun. Here we are still asleep.

The good news is that the mask is falling. The numbers are public, the departures are visible, the collapse of services is visible to the naked eye. Founders understand, young people understand, builders understand. We do not need more committees, more standards, or more people explaining why it is impossible. We need people who build: companies, technology, products, courage, truth. 1989 only killed the first form of communism. Today we are finally seeing the second, and now that we see it, we can fight it.
